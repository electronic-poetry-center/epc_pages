Protective Immediacy by Rod Smith

Rod Smith is his exact height and has basically quit playing golf. This precision and narrowing of focus permit him to arrange many parts of speech in interesting patterns, kept flat within this book but ready to spring out like so many Q-tipped Slinkies as the age-old hinge flexes. Working within the post-Olsonian precept "what does not explain is the will to explain", he destroyed the footnotes to this blurb before they were written. Too late!--the magnet hidden in these texts has already blanked your memory and begun to teach your thumbs to oppose again. --Tom Raworth

Rod Smith's Protective Immediacy is a wonderful book. It is also a worldly one. The works included here emerge from within a struggle--one with both political and personal overtones. But, though the struggle is in and of the world, it is undertaken without weariness; these poems are full of vivacity and often they are even hilarious. Propelled by a slightly hard-edge compassion, the writing sets aesthetic asymmetries against social asymmetries. In depicting the coexisting of these modes of asymmetry, Smith creates a provocative portrait of the world. Protective Immediacy is charged with life; the result is intelligent and thrilling. --Lyn Hejinian

This is a sophisticated poetry whose pleasures come from sounds felt right in the mouth while reading silently. And from the feeling, now and then, that one is exactly in among the gears of mind, however it works, what we don't know. --Alice Notley

 

Rod Smith was born in Gallipolis, Ohio in 1962, and grew up in Manassas, VA. He is the author of In Memory of My Theories, The Boy Poems, and The Lack (love poems, targets, flags . . . ). A postal worker for six years, he moved to Washington, DC in 1987. He is editor of the journal Aerial and publisher of Edge Books. Smith has coordinated reading series at Bick's Books, the District of Columbia Arts Center, and, currently, at Bridge Street Books in Georgetown.
